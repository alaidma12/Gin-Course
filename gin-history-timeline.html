c.1269
Netherlands
The Origin
Field Note · Netherlands · c.1269
Dutch distillers were producing a spirit called jenever — a grain-based alcohol flavored with juniper berries. At the time, juniper wasn't just a flavoring. It was believed to treat kidney ailments, stomach problems, and a general variety of things people didn't have better remedies for yet.

The name tells the story. Jenever comes from the Dutch word for juniper, from the French genièvre, from the Latin juniperus. Every language, every version of the word — it all comes back to the plant.
Barnett, R. (2011). The Book of Gin. Grove Press. · EU Regulation 110/2008 on spirit drinks.
1689
England
Dutch Courage
Field Note · England · 1689
English soldiers fighting alongside Dutch troops noticed something before battle — the Dutch drank jenever. It seemed to steady the nerves. The English adopted the habit, adopted the spirit, and brought both home when the wars ended.

When William of Orange became King of England in 1689, he banned the import of French brandy and encouraged domestic distillation from English grain. Almost overnight, the floodgates opened. Anyone could distill. Almost anyone did.
Barnett, R. (2011). The Book of Gin. Grove Press. · Dillon, P. (2002). The Much-Lamented Death of Madam Geneva. Review.
Field Note — Dutch Courage
"Dutch Courage" is a gin story. English soldiers picked up the habit from Dutch troops who drank jenever before battle. That phrase — and that spirit — crossed the channel together.
1743
London
The Gin Craze
Field Note · London · 1743
By 1743, London was consuming 10 litres of gin per person per year — men, women, and children included. Gin was cheap, unregulated, and available almost everywhere.

Parliament tried to slow things down with the 1736 Gin Act — extortionate license fees, heavy taxes on gin shops. It didn't work. Only two licenses were officially taken out. Production went underground instead, with bootleggers making gin in their own homes. That's where the phrase bathtub gin comes from.

The 1751 Gin Act took a different approach — lower fees, but distillers had to sell only to licensed retailers. A poor harvest that same year pushed grain prices up, which pushed gin prices up with them, putting it out of reach of the lower classes. By 1757 the craze was over. What remained was a trade that had started, slowly, to take quality seriously.

William Hogarth's engraving Gin Lane — depicting the city at its worst — was published in 1751, right at the turning point. It remains the defining image of the era.
Dillon, P. (2002). The Much-Lamented Death of Madam Geneva. Review. · Hogarth, W. (1751). Gin Lane [engraving]. · Barnett, R. (2011). The Book of Gin. Grove Press.
19th C
British India
The Gin & Tonic
Field Note · British India · 19th Century
The problem — Malaria

The treatment — Quinine — effective, deeply bitter

The solution — Mix with gin rations. Add citrus.

Medicine became something people looked forward to. The Gin and Tonic was born in the field, not a bar.

It is now one of the most ordered drinks in the world. Every time a beverage director puts a G&T on their menu — they're serving something with more history than most people realize.

That's a story worth telling.
Barnett, R. (2011). The Book of Gin. Grove Press. · Wondrich, D. (2007). Imbibe! Perigee Books.

The Revival
Field Note · Global · 21st Century
- Gin nearly disappears — outsold by vodka, seen as old-fashioned

- Early 2000s — craft distilleries begin opening across UK, US, Australia, Japan

- New botanicals, regional ingredients, Contemporary style emerges

- Japanese gin introduces an entirely different botanical vocabulary

- Butterfly pea flower turns gin into a tableside moment

The category didn't just survive. It reinvented itself.

Today there are over 500 gin distilleries in the UK alone — more variety, more story, and more conversation potential than almost anything else on a back bar.
