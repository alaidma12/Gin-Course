Three Methods. One Category.

Select a method to expand it and learn how it works.

i.
Pot Still Gin
Legal category: Distilled Gin
Traditional. Full-flavoured. Botanicals go in with the spirit.
What it is
The base spirit and botanicals go into a copper pot still together and are heated. As vapour rises, it carries the botanical character through the lyne arm — the curved pipe at the top — into a condenser, where it cools back into liquid. Rich, full-flavoured, and complex.
Vapour infusion — a variation
Some distillers suspend botanicals in a basket above the spirit rather than submerging them. The rising vapour passes through the basket, picking up flavour more gently — lighter extraction, more delicate result. Bombay Sapphire is the most well-known example.
Worth knowing
The oldest method — originally used for Dutch genever, the ancestor of modern gin. The heavier, maltier character of pot still gin is still present in some traditional Dutch and Belgian styles.
What it produces
Full-bodied gin with complex, layered botanical flavour. Juniper is forward. Each botanical expresses clearly.
What to tell a client
"Everything goes into the still together. You can taste every decision the distiller made."
Brand examples
Beefeater Tanqueray Bombay Sapphire*
On the label
Look for "Distilled Gin" — confirms the spirit went through a still with real botanicals, not extracts.
ii.
Column Still Gin
Legal category: Redistilled Gin
Cleaner. More precise. Neutral spirit redistilled with botanicals.
What it is
A neutral spirit is first macerated — steeped — with botanicals, then redistilled through a tall column still. The column refines the spirit multiple times in a single pass, producing a very clean, high-proof base. Botanical flavour is present but more precise and restrained than pot still gin.
Note on Hendrick's
Hendrick's runs both a pot still and a column still separately, then blends the outputs. The pot brings richness; the column brings precision. It sits between both worlds — which is exactly their point of difference.
Worth knowing
The column still transformed gin production in the 1830s. The lighter, drier style we associate with gin today is almost entirely a product of this method.
What it produces
Lighter, cleaner gin. The spirit steps back — the botanicals define the character. Consistent batch to batch.
What to tell a client
"The column strips everything back and lets the botanicals do the talking. Cleaner, crisper, more consistent."
Brand examples
Gordon's Sipsmith Hendrick's*
On the label
Look for "Redistilled Gin" or "London Dry Gin" — London Dry is always column still redistillation.
iii.
Compound Gin
Legal category: Compound Gin
No still. Botanical extracts blended directly into spirit.
What it is
No still involved. Botanical extracts or essences are blended directly into a neutral spirit and diluted to bottling strength. Faster and less expensive than distillation. Legal in most markets — but it cannot be labelled as a distilled gin.
Why it matters in sales
Knowing this distinction lets you position distilled gins confidently against less expensive alternatives — without being dismissive. It's a credibility moment with a knowledgeable client.
What it produces
Variable quality — can be perfectly serviceable or noticeably artificial depending on the extracts used. Rarely found at the premium end of the market.
What to tell a client
"No still, no heat — just extracts blended in. It's legal, but it's not the same process. Worth knowing when you're comparing bottles."
On the label
A bottle that says "Gin" without "distilled" may be compound gin — a useful distinction when a client asks why two bottles are at very different price points.
